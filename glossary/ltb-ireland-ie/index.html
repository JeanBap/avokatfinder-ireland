Loan-to-Value Limits Ireland

Ireland's Central Bank imposes binding loan-to-value (LTV) limits on mortgage lending — 90% for first-time buyers and 80% for second-time and subsequent buyers — to prevent excessive borrowing.

What Are LTV Limits?

The Central Bank of Ireland's mortgage measures include binding loan-to-value (LTV) limits that cap how much a bank can lend relative to the property's value. For first-time buyers (FTBs), the limit is 90% LTV — meaning a minimum 10% deposit is required. For second-time and subsequent buyers (SSBs), the limit is 80% LTV — requiring a 20% deposit. These limits were introduced in 2015 and revised in 2023 to make the framework more flexible while retaining its macroprudential core.

Loan-to-Income (LTI) Limits

Ireland's mortgage measures also include a loan-to-income (LTI) limit: mortgage lending is generally capped at 4x gross income (single applicant) or 4x combined gross income (couple). Banks may exceed this threshold for up to 20% of the value of new mortgage lending in each calendar year, allowing some flexibility for higher earners. In practice, banks exercise this allowance cautiously and typically for strong credit profiles.

First Home Scheme and Help to Buy

Ireland operates two state schemes to help buyers assemble deposits: the First Home Scheme (shared equity between the state and the buyer on new builds) and the Help to Buy scheme (income tax rebate of up to €30,000 for first-time buyers purchasing new-build properties). These schemes are designed to work alongside the LTV limits by supplementing deposits without increasing overall mortgage debt. Buyers should model both schemes to see which provides the greater benefit for their situation.

Exceptions for Construction

Borrowers constructing their own home (self-build) face a different LTV calculation as the property value is determined at the land acquisition and construction cost stages separately. Self-build mortgages are staged drawdowns rather than lump-sum advances. First-time buyer LTV limits still apply but the practical mechanics differ from a standard purchase.

2023 Reforms

The Central Bank's 2023 review maintained the core LTV and LTI limits but introduced more flexibility in how banks can use their allowances, and strengthened the LTI limit for SSBs (moving from 3.5x to 4x to align with FTBs). The review also introduced a new first-home buyer 80% LTV exemption for green mortgages, acknowledging the energy performance of new builds.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Relying on a verbal 'pre-approval in principle' from a bank before bidding on a property. The LTV and LTI limits mean the actual approved amount may be lower than the indicative figure — always get a formal approval in writing.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: If you are close to the LTI ceiling, a salary increase (even a modest one confirmed by a P60) between application and formal offer can materially increase the approved loan — timing your mortgage application matters.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use gifted funds for my deposit?

Yes — Irish banks accept gifted deposits (typically from parents) but require a gift letter confirming no repayment obligation.

Does the LTV limit apply to investment mortgages?

Investment mortgages in Ireland face separate rules — typically 70% LTV maximum for residential investment properties.

What is the Central Bank's rationale for these limits?

To prevent a repeat of the pre-2008 credit bubble by ensuring borrowers have real equity in their homes and can service debt at higher interest rates.
