Capital Gains Tax

Capital Gains Tax Ireland – Property CGT Rates and Exemptions

Capital Gains Tax (CGT) in Ireland is charged on gains arising from the disposal of assets, including property. The standard CGT rate is 33%, applied to the net gain after deducting allowable costs. For property owners, CGT is one of the most significant tax considerations when selling an investment or second property.

The taxable gain is the proceeds minus the original acquisition cost, incidental costs of purchase and sale (such as legal fees, estate agent fees, and stamp duty paid), and enhancement expenditure (capital improvements). Maintenance and repairs are not deductible. An annual small-gains exemption of €1,270 is available to individuals.

The most important exemption is the Principal Private Residence (PPR) relief. If the property was your only or main home throughout the period of ownership, the entire gain is exempt from CGT. If you lived in it for only part of the ownership period, a time-based apportionment applies: the fraction of time lived in as PPR is exempt, plus a final 12 months deemed occupation.

CGT is payable in two tranches: gains arising January to November are payable by 15 December; gains arising in December are payable by 31 January of the following year. The relevant CGT return must be filed by 31 October of the year following the disposal. Missing these deadlines triggers interest (currently 0.0219% per day).

Non-resident sellers of Irish property are subject to a withholding tax mechanism. The buyer is required to withhold 15% of the consideration and remit it to Revenue unless the seller has obtained a CG50A clearance certificate from Revenue in advance. Non-residents should apply for the CG50A well before completion.

⚠ Common MistakeAssuming PPR relief applies automatically to a property that was once your home. If you rented it out for several years before selling, only the owner-occupied period plus 12 months qualifies for PPR relief; the rest is fully chargeable.
💡 Expert TipIf you are selling an investment property, time the transaction carefully. Completing before December avoids the compressed December CGT payment window and gives you until 15 December of the disposal year to pay.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is CGT payable on inherited property in Ireland?

No CGT arises on inheritance itself, but if you later sell inherited property, CGT is calculated on the gain from the date-of-death value (the probate value) to the sale price.

Can losses be offset against property CGT?

Yes. Capital losses from other disposals in the same tax year can be offset against gains before computing CGT. Unused losses can be carried forward indefinitely.

Does PPR relief apply to a property held in a company?

No. PPR relief is available only to individuals. Property held in a company structure is subject to different tax rules.
