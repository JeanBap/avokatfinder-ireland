Stamp Duty Ireland

Stamp duty in Ireland is a 1% tax on residential property purchases up to €1 million and 2% on the balance above, payable by the buyer and collected by Revenue at closing.

Residential Stamp Duty Rates

Ireland's stamp duty on residential property is levied at 1% on the first €1 million of the purchase price and 2% on any excess above €1 million. For example, on a €1.2 million purchase: 1% × €1m = €10,000 plus 2% × €200,000 = €4,000 = €14,000 total. Non-residential property (commercial, agricultural) is taxed at 7.5% (increased from 6% in Budget 2020). Stamp duty is the buyer's obligation and is paid to Revenue via the solicitor at closing.

New Builds and Approved Housing Bodies

Stamp duty is calculated on the VAT-exclusive price for new residential properties — VAT (13.5% for residential construction) is excluded from the stamp duty base. On a €400,000 new apartment where VAT is included, stamp duty is calculated on the net-of-VAT price. Transfers to approved housing bodies (social housing providers) attract a reduced 1% rate across the full price.

Consanguinity Relief

Transfers of non-residential property between certain family members (consanguinity relief) attract a halved stamp duty rate — currently 3.75% instead of 7.5%. This applies to agricultural land and commercial property transferred between parents, children, siblings, and certain other relatives. Residential property does not benefit from consanguinity relief on stamp duty (though Capital Acquisitions Tax reliefs may apply).

Refund Scheme for Rental Properties

A stamp duty refund scheme allows developers who pay stamp duty on non-residential land that they subsequently develop for housing to claim a refund when the residential units are completed and sold. This avoids double-charging stamp duty on land that is converted to residential use and was introduced to incentivise housing development.

Filing and Payment

Stamp duty must be filed and paid within 44 days of the completion date (the day of closing). Late filing attracts penalties and interest. The solicitor typically files the stamp duty return (eStamping) on the buyer's behalf and retains stamp duty funds from the completion proceeds. Buyers should budget for stamp duty well in advance — on a €500,000 purchase it is €5,000 due on the closing day.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Forgetting that stamp duty is due on the closing day — not weeks later. Your solicitor needs the funds in the client account before they can complete. Confirm the exact stamp duty amount well before closing.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: For new-build purchases, confirm with your solicitor whether VAT is included or excluded in the quoted price — stamp duty is on the net-of-VAT figure, which can be significantly lower than the headline price.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is stamp duty refundable if the sale falls through?

If the transaction does not complete, stamp duty is refundable — your solicitor applies to Revenue for the refund.

Does stamp duty apply to shared ownership schemes?

Yes — but only on the share being purchased, not the total market value of the property.

Can stamp duty be mortgaged?

Irish lenders typically do not include stamp duty in the mortgage — it must be funded from your own resources alongside the deposit.
