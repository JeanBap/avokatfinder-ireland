Building Survey Ireland

A building survey (or structural survey) in Ireland is a professional inspection of a property's physical condition, strongly recommended for any second-hand purchase before contracts are signed.

Why a Survey Is Essential in Ireland

Ireland operates on a strict caveat emptor (buyer beware) principle — sellers are not required to disclose physical defects, and the buyer takes the property in the condition in which they find it unless specific warranties are negotiated. A professional building survey is therefore the buyer's primary protection against latent defects in the structure, fabric, or services of a second-hand property.

Types of Survey in Ireland

The main options are: a Mortgage Valuation (carried out by the bank for its own security — not a structural assessment and not shared in detail with the buyer); a Snag List (for new builds — checking contractor defects before closing); and a full Building Survey (commissioned by the buyer, covering structure, damp, roof, services, boundaries, and planning). For most second-hand purchases, a full building survey by a Chartered Building Surveyor (SCSI/RICS) is recommended.

Common Irish Property Issues

Irish surveyors frequently encounter: pyrite in concrete floors (especially in Leinster — a structural and insurance-impacting issue); mica in concrete blocks in Donegal, Clare, and Mayo; radon gas (a radioactive gas from ground — a health risk in certain areas requiring mitigation); planning compliance issues (extensions without permission); and older properties with outdated electrical wiring, septic tank conditions (in rural areas), and flat-roof extensions with waterproofing failure.

Pyrite and Mica — Special Considerations

Pyrite (iron sulphide in hardcore fill under concrete floors, causing heave and cracking) and mica (expansive aggregate in concrete blocks causing external cracking) are nationally significant issues in Ireland. Both are remediation schemes funded by the state. A surveyor's report should specifically address pyrite/mica risk — a positive indication triggers further specialist investigation and potentially significantly affects value and insurability.

When to Commission a Survey

In Ireland, surveys are typically done before contracts are exchanged — not after. The standard advice is to commission a survey immediately after an offer is accepted (or even before bidding on a property likely to sell quickly). Survey results can be used to renegotiate the price or withdraw before contracts are signed. Once contracts are exchanged, the buyer has committed.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Relying on the bank's mortgage valuation as a substitute for a structural survey. The bank's valuer is assessing security value, not your protection — they will not inspect for pyrite, mica, or radon.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: For any property in a county with known pyrite or mica risk (Dublin, Meath, Wicklow, Donegal, Clare, Mayo), ask your surveyor explicitly to assess for these issues — they require a specific inspection protocol.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a building survey cost in Ireland?

Typically €350–€600 for a standard residential property — more for larger or older properties.

Can a survey result in renegotiating the price?

Yes — a survey revealing significant defects is commonly used to renegotiate. Sellers typically accept a reduction rather than lose the sale.

Do I need a survey for a new-build?

A snagging list rather than a full structural survey is standard for new builds. However, some buyers also commission a structural inspection of the building itself, not just internal finishes.
