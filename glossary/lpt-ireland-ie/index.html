Local Property Tax Ireland

Local Property Tax (LPT) is Ireland's annual tax on residential properties, self-assessed by the owner based on the property's market value band, with rates set by each local authority.

What Is Local Property Tax?

Ireland's Local Property Tax (LPT) was introduced in 2013 and is an annual self-assessed tax on the market value of all residential properties in the State. Unlike some European countries where property tax is based on an outdated notional value, Irish LPT is recalculated every 4 years based on current market values. The most recent revaluation was in November 2021, effective from 2022, establishing the current valuation bands.

Valuation Bands and Rates

Properties are placed in valuation bands (e.g. Band 1: up to €200,000; Band 2: €200,001–€262,500; etc.) and taxed at a rate applied to the midpoint of the band. The basic rate from 2022 is 0.1029% of the band midpoint value. Local authorities can vary this rate by up to 15% above or below the basic rate. LPT is paid annually — typically via direct debit, single payment, or deduction at source from income or pension.

Liability and Property Sales

The person liable for LPT is the owner of the property on 1 November of the preceding year. When a property is sold during the year, the seller is typically liable for the full year's LPT (having been the owner on the liability date). In practice, purchase contracts in Ireland include a pro-rata LPT adjustment so the buyer compensates the seller for the portion of the year from closing to year-end. Always confirm this adjustment is in the contract.

Exemptions

LPT exemptions include: newly built properties (exempt until end of 2025 for properties built between 1 January 2013 and 31 October 2021); properties certified as structurally unsound or having significant pyrite/mica damage; properties owned by charities; and specific diplomatic property. First-time buyers should check if the property they are purchasing is currently in an LPT exemption period — it affects their future liability.

Compliance and Revenue

Revenue collects LPT and enforcement has become increasingly robust. Outstanding LPT is a charge on the property and must be cleared before a transfer can be registered. The Property Services Regulatory Authority requires LPT compliance evidence on all property transactions — a solicitor completing a purchase will verify LPT is current and will not close without this confirmation.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Buying a property mid-year assuming zero LPT liability. If the seller was the owner on 1 November, they owe the full year — but your contract must clearly pro-rate this or the seller may retain the full amount.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Check the LPT band for every property you are considering — Revenue's online LPT calculator lets you enter the address and see the current band and annual liability before you make an offer.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does LPT apply to rented properties?

Yes — the owner (landlord) is liable for LPT regardless of whether the property is rented.

Can I pay LPT in instalments?

Yes — Revenue offers monthly direct debit, single payment, or deduction at source from employment income.

What if I disagree with my property's valuation band?

You can submit a self-assessment at a different band via Revenue Online Service (ROS) — you are legally responsible for the accuracy of your own valuation.
